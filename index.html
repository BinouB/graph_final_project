About the SEN‑B Project

The SEN‑B cohort follows nearly 1,000 adults with HBV mono‑infection or HIV/HBV co‑infection at the Infectious and Tropical Diseases Unit (SMIT) of Fann University Hospital (CHNU Fann) in Dakar, Senegal.

Specific Objectives

  • Evaluate eligibility for antiviral treatment and monitor clinical & virological response.
  • Determine hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) incidence and develop a predictive screening score.
  • Investigate intra‑hepatic determinants of HBV and validate peripheral biomarkers of cccDNA.
  • Assess HBV knowledge and treatment perceptions via socio‑anthropological study.
  • Monitor SARS‑CoV‑2 seroprevalence trends within the cohort.

About Hepatitis B

Key Facts

  • Hepatitis B is a viral infection that attacks the liver and can cause both acute and chronic disease.
  • Transmission occurs most commonly from mother to child, in early childhood, or via contact with infected blood/body fluids.
  • WHO estimates 254 million people lived with chronic HBV in 2022; ~1.2 million new infections each year.
  • In 2022, HBV caused ≈1.1 million deaths (mostly cirrhosis or HCC).
  • Safe and effective vaccines are available.

Overview

Hepatitis B is a liver infection caused by HBV. It can be acute (short‑term) or chronic (long‑term) and may lead to cirrhosis or liver cancer. The virus spreads through infectious body fluids (blood, saliva, vaginal secretions, semen) and perinatal transmission. Vaccination shortly after birth offers near‑complete protection.

Highest burden: Western Pacific (97 M) and Africa (65 M); South‑East Asia 61 M, Eastern Mediterranean 15 M, Europe 11 M, Americas 5 M.

Transmission

  • Perinatal & early‑childhood exposure in high‑endemic areas.
  • Needle sticks, tattoos, piercings, reuse of contaminated equipment.
  • Unprotected sex with an infected partner.
  • Virus survives ≥7 days outside body; incubation 30–180 days.
  • Chronic infection: <5% in adults, ~95% in infants → vaccination priority.

Symptoms

  • Often asymptomatic when newly infected.
  • Possible acute illness: jaundice, dark urine, fatigue, nausea, abdominal pain.
  • Severe cases can progress to liver failure or death.

HBV–HIV Coinfection

~1 % of people with HBV (≈2.7 M) also have HIV; conversely HBV prevalence among people with HIV is 7.4 %. Tenofovir‑based ART treats both infections.

Diagnosis

  • Laboratory tests differentiate acute vs. chronic infection and assess liver damage.
  • WHO: test all donated blood; only 13 % of people with HBV knew their status in 2022, 3 % were on treatment.
  • Testing priorities: blood donors, pregnant women, high‑risk groups.

Treatment

  • No specific therapy for acute HBV; supportive care.
  • Chronic HBV: oral antivirals (tenofovir, entecavir) slow cirrhosis, cut cancer risk, require lifelong adherence.
  • WHO guidelines: >50 % of chronic patients should receive treatment, context dependent.

Prevention

  • Birth‑dose vaccine within 24 h, then 2–3 doses ≥4 weeks apart.
  • Protection ≥20 years, likely lifelong; boosters generally unnecessary.
  • Antivirals + vaccine prevent mother‑to‑child transmission.
  • Safer sex, needle safety, hand hygiene, HCW vaccination.

WHO Action

The Global Health Sector Strategies (2022–2030) aim to eliminate HIV, viral hepatitis, and STIs by 2030. World Hepatitis Day 2023 theme: “One Life, One Liver”.
