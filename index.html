About the SEN‑B Project

The SEN‑B project follows a cohort of nearly 1,000 adult patients with HBV monoinfection or HIV/HBV coinfection at the Infectious and Tropical Diseases Unit (SMIT) of Fann University Hospital (CHNU) in Dakar, Senegal.

Specific Objectives

  • Evaluate eligibility for HBV antiviral treatment and monitor clinical and virological responses.
  • Determine hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) incidence and develop a predictive score to guide screening.
  • Investigate intra‑hepatic histopathological determinants and validate peripheral cccDNA biomarkers.
  • Assess knowledge and perceptions of HBV treatment through a socio‑anthropological study.
  • Monitor SARS‑CoV‑2 seroprevalence trends among people living with HBV in the cohort.

About Hepatitis B

Key Facts

  • Hepatitis B is a viral infection that attacks the liver and can cause both acute and chronic disease.
  • The virus is most commonly transmitted from mother to child at birth, in early childhood, or through contact with blood or other body fluids during unprotected sex, unsafe injections, or exposure to sharp instruments.
  • In 2022, an estimated 254 million people were living with chronic hepatitis B, with 1.2 million new infections each year.
  • Approximately 1.1 million deaths occurred in 2022, mainly from cirrhosis or hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Safe and effective vaccines are available to prevent hepatitis B.

Overview

Hepatitis B is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV). The infection can be acute (short‑term) or chronic (long‑term) and can lead to cirrhosis or liver cancer. The virus spreads through contact with infected body fluids (blood, saliva, vaginal secretions, semen) and from mother to infant at birth. Vaccination shortly after birth, followed by booster doses, provides near‑complete protection. The Western Pacific and African regions bear the highest burden, with millions of chronic infections worldwide.

  • Western Pacific: 97 million chronically infected
  • African Region: 65 million
  • South‑East Asia: 61 million
  • Eastern Mediterranean: 15 million
  • European Region: 11 million
  • Americas: 5 million

Transmission

High‑endemic areas: HBV is most often transmitted from mother to child (perinatal) or through horizontal exposure to infected blood in early childhood. Chronic infection develops in ~95 % of infants infected this way, versus <5 % of adults.

  • Needle sticks, tattoos, piercings
  • Exposure to infected blood or body fluids (saliva, menstrual blood, vaginal secretions, semen)
  • Reuse of contaminated needles/syringes in healthcare settings or among people who inject drugs
  • Sexual transmission (higher risk in unvaccinated individuals with multiple partners)

HBV can survive outside the body for at least 7 days. Incubation: 30–180 days; the virus becomes detectable 30–60 days post‑infection and may persist, leading to chronic hepatitis B.

Symptoms

Most newly infected individuals are asymptomatic. Acute symptoms (lasting weeks) can include jaundice, dark urine, fatigue, nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Severe acute hepatitis can lead to liver failure and death. Chronic hepatitis B may progress to cirrhosis or liver cancer.

HBV–HIV Coinfection

  • ~1 % of people with HBV (≈2.7 million) are also infected with HIV.
  • Global HBV prevalence among people with HIV: 7.4 %.
  • WHO recommends HIV treatment for all diagnosed individuals; tenofovir in first‑line regimens is also active against HBV.

Diagnosis

HBV cannot be distinguished from other hepatitis viruses by symptoms alone; laboratory testing is essential. Blood tests determine acute vs. chronic infection and monitor liver damage. Liver fibrosis can be assessed by clinical exam, ultrasound, or elastography. All donated blood should be tested for HBV.

  • 2022: Only 13 % of people with HBV knew their status; 3 % (7 million) were on treatment.
  • Chronic HBV in children under 5 has dropped from ≈5 % (pre‑vaccine era) to <1 % in 2019.

Testing recommendations: In areas with >2 % HBsAg prevalence, offer universal adult testing. Prioritize blood donors, pregnant women, and high‑risk groups (migrants from endemic areas, close contacts, healthcare workers, people who inject drugs, prisoners, men who have sex with men, sex workers, people with HIV).

Treatment

No specific therapy exists for acute hepatitis B; care focuses on comfort, hydration, and nutrition. Chronic HBV can be treated with oral antivirals such as tenofovir or entecavir, which slow cirrhosis, reduce liver cancer risk, and improve survival. Most patients require lifelong therapy. Updated WHO guidelines recommend treatment for >50 % of chronic HBV cases, depending on context.

Liver cancer management: In low‑income settings, most patients present late and die within months. In high‑income countries, earlier detection allows surgery, chemotherapy, or liver transplantation.

Prevention

  • Birth dose of HBV vaccine within 24 hours, followed by 2–3 additional doses ≥4 weeks apart.
  • Booster doses generally unnecessary after the 3‑dose series; protection lasts ≥20 years, likely lifelong.
  • Antiviral therapy plus vaccination prevents mother‑to‑child transmission.
  • Safer sex practices, avoiding needle sharing, proper hand hygiene, and vaccination of healthcare workers further reduce transmission.

WHO Action

The Global Health Sector Strategies on HIV, Viral Hepatitis, and STIs (2022–2030) aim to eliminate chronic HBV and HCV as public health threats by 2030, emphasizing prevention, testing, and treatment with a focus on high‑risk populations and inequities. World Hepatitis Day 2023 adopted the theme "One Life, One Liver" to underscore liver health and the urgency of scaling up HBV elimination efforts.
